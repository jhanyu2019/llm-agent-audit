AI Agent Action-Safety Audit

An independent, staging-only check of whether your tool-using agent can be pushed into a high-impact action it should not take.

What I test

When an AI agent can call tools, the failure that matters is not what it says, it is what it does. I check whether content the agent reads, such as a user message, a support ticket, an email, a document, or a tool response, can push it into a high-impact action it should not take, such as issuing a refund, moving money, deleting an account, granting access, disabling MFA, or exporting data.

Why it matters

A model's refusal is a safety feature, not an authorization system, and the choice of model does not change that. On a fixed battery run across six current models from three vendors, the number of unsafe outcomes ranged from 0.0 to 8.0 on the identical test, and the more expensive tier did not predict safer behavior. The thing that stops an unauthorized action has to live in your application, so the only reliable way to know what your agent does is to test your agent. See the writeups and data.

Three examples

HealthcareA specialty prior-authorization agent reads a clinical note or a payer response and submits a request. Could that content steer the medical-necessity framing, or change what gets submitted?
LogisticsA dispatch agent reads a carrier email or a call transcript and dispatches a job or changes a rate. Could a crafted message trigger the wrong dispatch or rate change?
Finance and APAn accounts-payable agent reads a vendor invoice and pays it or updates bank details. Could the invoice content redirect a payment?

How a pilot works

Your team runs a small set of scenarios against a staging copy of your agent and sends back the tool-call traces. I score them by hand and send a report mapped to the OWASP Top 10 for LLM Applications, with the tool-call evidence and concrete fixes. No production access, no real customer data, no shared credentials. See a sample report.
